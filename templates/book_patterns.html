Gutta-percha golf balls came in various sizes, weights, and cover patterns. Understanding these characteristics helps with identification and dating of specimens.

Cover Patterns

Hand Hammered

The earliest pattern, created by hammering the gutta-percha with a specially shaped hammer. Each ball is unique. Associated with the 1840s-1860s.

Moulded Mesh

The most common pattern, featuring a grid-like design impressed during moulding. Produced from the 1860s through the 1890s.

Bramble

A pattern of small raised bumps resembling a raspberry or brambleberry. Patented in 1895 and popular through 1903.

Machine Cut

Precise geometric patterns cut by machine rather than moulded. Late 1880s-1890s.

Smooth

Unpatterned balls, often early experimental pieces or practice balls.

Specialty Patterns

Unique designs patented by specific manufacturers, such as rings of stars, diamond meshes, or proprietary textures.

Sizes and Weights

Gutta-percha balls were typically measured in pennyweights (dwt) and grains:

Size Weight Era
25 ~25 dwt 1850s (rare)
27 ~27 dwt (418 grains) 1860s-1870s
27½ ~27.5 dwt (426 grains) 1880s-1890s (most common)
28 ~28 dwt (433 grains) Late 1890s
Note: 1 pennyweight (dwt) = 24 grains = 1.555 grams. The 27½ size became the standard for gutta-percha balls in the 1880s and remained so until the introduction of rubber-cored balls.

Pricing in the Era

Original prices for gutta-percha balls varied by quality and brand:

  • Budget balls: 1 shilling (1/-) per ball
  • Standard quality: 1 shilling 6 pence (1/6d) per ball
  • Premium balls: 2 shillings (2/-) or more per ball
  • By the dozen: Discounts available, typically 10-12 shillings per dozen

Moulds for making balls at home cost approximately £5 in the 1870s-1880s—a significant investment equivalent to several months' wages for a working man.
